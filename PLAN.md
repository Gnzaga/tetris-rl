## 1. Research grounding — why this design (do not re-litigate during implementation)

- **Frame-level RL on Tetris fails; placement-level decisions with afterstate values succeed.** Actions are "choose (rotation, column) for the current piece"; a deterministic controller handles frame-level animation. Afterstate value functions outperform state-action Q formulations on Tetris because the state-action space and delayed rewards are otherwise intractable (Bdolah & Livnat lineage; Lundgaard & McKee; confirmed repeatedly in the literature and in practical repos like nuno-faria/tetris-ai, which scores enumerated next-states with a value net).
- **The strongest known Tetris controllers are linear evaluations over hand-crafted features, tuned by the cross-entropy method.** Dellacherie's hand-weighted 6-feature controller averages ~660,000 lines (Fahey 2003). Szita & Lőrincz (2006, Neural Computation) reached ~350,000 lines with noisy CEM over Bertsekas features. Thiery & Scherrer (2009, ICGA Journal; 2008 RL Competition winner) combined Dellacherie's features with two of their own ("BCTS"), tuned by CEM, reaching ~35,000,000 lines on the standard research variant. Approximate DP methods (CBMPI, Scherrer et al.) are the other strong family.
- **Consequence for this project:** the CEM + BCTS-features agent is cheap to train (minutes), near-SOTA, and serves three roles: (a) engine-correctness canary via Dellacherie's published hand weights, (b) optional behavior-cloning teacher to warm-start the neural net, (c) a strong "classical baseline" entry in the demo dropdown. The neural afterstate ValueNet is the ML centerpiece — it learns from the raw board with no hand-crafted features. Expect it to play well (thousands to hundreds of thousands of lines) but **below** the tuned linear agent; that is the honest, well-documented state of the field and the demo should present both.
- **Reward shaping:** pure lines-cleared is sparse; superlinear multi-line bonuses plus small annealed shaping penalties (holes, height) are standard practice for making learning tractable, with shaping annealed to zero so the true objective dominates at convergence.

References (for the human, not for you to fetch): Fahey, "Tetris AI" (colinfahey.com, 2003) · Szita & Lőrincz, "Learning Tetris Using the Noisy Cross-Entropy Method," Neural Computation 18(12), 2006 · Thiery & Scherrer, "Improvements on Learning Tetris with Cross Entropy," ICGA Journal 32(1), 2009 (hal: inria-00418930) · Gabillon, Ghavamzadeh, Scherrer, "Approximate Dynamic Programming Finally Performs Well in the Game of Tetris," NeurIPS 2013 · github.com/nuno-faria/tetris-ai.

## 2. Frozen game-rules spec (identical in Python and JS)

**Board.** 10 columns × 20 rows. Row 0 is the top, row 19 the bottom. Python engine stores rows as 16-bit integers (bit c set = cell (row, c) filled); JS may use arrays or bitmasks, but transitions must be identical.

**Pieces.** The 7 tetrominoes (I, O, T, S, Z, J, L). `shared/pieces.json` defines, per piece, a list of **distinct** rotation states (O: 1; I, S, Z: 2; T, J, L: 4). Each rotation state is a list of `[row, col]` cell offsets from the top-left of its bounding box, plus the state's `width` and `height`. Use SRS-canonical shapes. This JSON is loaded by both engines — never duplicate the tables in code.

**RNG.** mulberry32, seeded with a uint32. `next_float() = next_uint32() / 2^32`. Python must mask to 32 bits at every step to match JS `>>> 0` semantics exactly.

**7-bag randomizer.** Refill a bag with piece indices [0..6], Fisher-Yates shuffle using exactly: `for i from 6 down to 1: j = floor(next_float() * (i + 1)); swap(bag[i], bag[j])`. Draw from the front. The engine exposes a lookahead queue of the next 5 pieces (demo preview; the agent may only use the current piece in v1).

**Action space = placements.** A placement is `(rotation_index, column)` where `column ∈ [0, 10 − width]`. The piece drops straight down from above the board to its resting position (highest row where it collides with the stack or floor, minus one). A placement is **legal** iff every cell of the resting piece has `row ≥ 0`. There is no per-frame gravity, soft-drop, tuck, kick, or hold in the engine — the engine's only transition is `step(rotation, column)`, applied atomically. (The browser's spawn/rotate/move/drop animation is presentation-only and never touches engine state — this is what guarantees sim parity.)

**Step semantics.** `step(r, c)`: lock the piece; compute `landing_height` and `eroded_piece_cells` (see §features) before clearing; remove full rows, shifting rows above down; draw the next piece from the bag; game over if the **new** current piece has zero legal placements OR the attempted placement was illegal (trainers/agents must only choose from `legal_placements()`; the illegal case exists for safety).

**Per-placement info returned by step:** `lines_cleared`, `game_over`, plus the feature/shaping raw materials below.

**Scoring (display + base reward):** `clear_points = [0, 1, 3, 5, 8]` indexed by lines cleared. Demo displays `lines` and `score = Σ clear_points × 100`.

**Training reward:** `r = clear_points[lines] − β · (0.3 · max(0, Δholes) + 0.05 · max(0, Δmax_height))`, terminal transitions add `−10` and do not bootstrap. β anneals linearly 1 → 0 over the first 60% of the TD training budget. CEM ignores shaping (its fitness is raw lines).

**Features (BCTS set, 8 values; the frozen definitions):** computed at `step` time. (1) `landing_height`: mean of (rows-from-bottom of the piece's lowest and highest cells), measured **before** clears, where rows-from-bottom = 20 − row. (2) `eroded_piece_cells`: lines cleared this step × number of this piece's cells that were in those cleared lines. (3) `row_transitions`: over the post-clear board, count horizontal filled↔empty adjacencies per row; both side walls count as filled. (4) `column_transitions`: vertical filled↔empty adjacencies per column; the floor counts as filled, the cell above row 0 counts as empty. (5) `holes`: empty cells with at least one filled cell above them in the same column. (6) `cumulative_wells`: for every maximal vertical run of empty cells whose left and right neighbors are both filled (walls count as filled), add `1 + 2 + … + depth`. (7) `hole_depth`: sum over all holes of the number of filled cells directly above that hole. (8) `rows_with_holes`: number of rows containing ≥1 hole. A linear agent's score for a placement is `w · f`, maximized over legal placements. **Dellacherie's published hand weights** for features 1–6 (with 7–8 zeroed) are `[-1, +1, -1, -1, -4, -1]` — ship these as a built-in agent.

## 8. Phase 5 — Neural afterstate ValueNet + TD training (the ML centerpiece)

`tetris/model.py` — **ValueNet**: input `[B, 1, 20, 10]` float32 binary boards → Conv(1→16, 3×3, pad 1)+ReLU → Conv(16→32, 3×3, pad 1)+ReLU → Conv(32→32, 3×3, pad 1)+ReLU → Flatten → FC(32·200→128)+ReLU → FC(128→1). (~0.9M params, ~3.5 MB fp32 — comfortable for the browser.) Grid-only input; no hand features (that's the point).

**Decision rule** (train and demo, identical): for the current piece, enumerate legal placements; for each, form the pre-clear reward `r_i = clear_points[lines_i] − shaping_i` and afterstate board `A_i` (post-clear); score `q_i = r_i + γ · V(A_i)` with γ = 0.95, batching all candidates in **one** forward pass; act greedily (ε-greedy during training).

`tetris/td.py` — synchronous vectorized self-play in one process: 64 parallel game states; each step, concatenate all envs' candidate afterstates into one forward (~64 × ~34 ≈ 2k boards/batch). Store transitions `(A_k, r_{k+1}, A_{k+1}, done)` — the TD(0) afterstate target is `V(A_k) ← r_{k+1} + γ · V̄(A_{k+1})` with a target network `V̄` (hard-copy every 2,500 updates), no bootstrap on terminal (terminal r includes the −10 death penalty). Replay buffer 500k (store boards as 20 uint16 rows, decode on sample); batch 512; Huber loss; Adam 3e-4; ~1 gradient update per vector-env step; ε linear 1.0 → 0.02 over the first 30% of the budget; β shaping anneal per §2. Default budget: 2,000,000 placements. Eval every 50,000 placements: 20 greedy games, cap 10,000 pieces, fixed seeds; log per §6 and save a replay.

**`--warm-start` (recommended default):** before TD, roll out the CEM agent for 200,000 placements; at each decision, record all candidate afterstates with the teacher's linear scores normalized per-decision (zero mean, unit variance); pretrain V by MSE regression for 3 epochs; then start TD with ε = 0.2 instead of 1.0. This skips the random-flailing phase.

**Milestone checkpoints for the demo (mandatory):** save `nn_step_0` (untrained) and checkpoints nearest 10%, 30%, 60%, 100% of budget.

**Gates:**
1. `python scripts/train_td.py --smoke` (tiny net budget, ~2 min) end-to-end green, run dir well-formed, loss finite and decreasing.
2. Full run (`--run-name td_v1 --warm-start`): final greedy eval **median ≥ 2,000 lines** (20 games, 10k-piece cap → report lines-at-cap if capped). Stretch (report, don't block): ≥ 20,000. It will likely trail `cem_v1` — expected and fine; say so in the final report rather than chasing it.
3. Monotonic-ish learning curve: eval median at 100% > at 30% > at 0%.

Expected wall clock: ~20 min smoke-scale sanity; 2–8 h on an 8-core CPU for the full budget. Print a clear ETA so the human can decide when to launch the long run.

## 10. Phase 7 — Browser demo (static, local)

Plain HTML/CSS/ES-modules in `demo/`, served with `python -m http.server 8000` **from the repo root** (so `/demo/` and `/runs/` are both reachable). No build step.

**Layout & behavior:**
- Canvas board (24 px cells) + 5-piece preview queue + a hidden 3-row area above the board where spawn/rotate/translate animation happens.
- **Game loop:** `requestAnimationFrame` with a fixed 33.33 ms accumulator (30 Hz logic). The **controller** (`controller.js`) is presentation-only: when the agent picks `(r, c)`, animate rotation then horizontal moves at 1 action/tick in the hidden rows, then descend 3 rows/tick to the resting position, then call the engine's atomic `step(r, c)`. Engine state is never derived from animation.
- **Agent runner:** on piece spawn, enumerate placements via the JS engine; linear agents score via `features.js`; ONNX agents batch all candidate boards into one `ort` inference call. Record decision latency.
- **Controls:** agent/checkpoint dropdown (from manifest, showing eval stats), speed 1× / 4× / 20× / MAX (MAX skips animation, steps the engine directly, renders every Nth piece), pause / step / restart, seed input.
- **The ML-visible layer (this is the portfolio point):** (a) heatmap toggle — ghost outlines of the top-5 candidate placements tinted by softmax-normalized score with the value printed beside each; (b) stats panel — lines, pieces, PPS, rolling-average decision ms, model params + file size; (c) a small canvas line chart of the training curve from `manifest.curve`; (d) Replay tab — dropdown of `runs/*` (probe known run names or a `runs/index.json` written by trainers), lists `replays/index.json`, animates any eval replay through the same engine; "live" toggle re-polls every 10 s.
